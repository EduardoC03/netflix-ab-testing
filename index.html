01 — The Business Problem

Netflix Doesn't Sell Movies.
It Sells Clicks.

Every time you open Netflix, you are not actually watching anything yet — you are scrolling. In those few seconds of browsing, the only thing standing between a subscriber and a viewing session is a thumbnail image. Netflix has roughly 1–2 seconds to convince you that a title is worth your time before you scroll past it forever.

This creates a very specific and measurable business problem: which image for a given title generates the most clicks? The answer is not obvious. A dramatic scene might appeal to one viewer while a character-focused close-up resonates more with another. Guessing wrong means missed viewing hours. At scale — with over 300 million subscribers — even a small improvement in click rate translates into millions of additional viewing sessions, stronger engagement, and lower subscriber churn.

Netflix's solution is to stop guessing entirely and let data make the decision. That process is called A/B testing, and it is the foundation of how Netflix makes artwork decisions at scale.

02 — The Method

What Is A/B Testing — And Why Does It Matter?

An A/B test is a controlled business experiment. Two versions of something — a thumbnail, a price, a button color, a subject line — are shown to different randomly selected groups of users at the same time. Because the groups are random and the only difference between them is the thing being tested, any measurable gap in behavior can be attributed to that one change.

The critical word is random. If Netflix accidentally showed Artwork B only to their most engaged subscribers, the result would be meaningless. Randomization ensures a fair comparison — both groups are drawn from the same general population, so the artwork itself is the only variable that can explain a difference in clicks.

A/B testing sits at the intersection of business strategy and machine learning. In Netflix's real system, these experiments feed data into AI models that learn which artwork characteristics — facial expressions, color palettes, scene intensity — predict clicks for specific audience segments. The test is not just a one-time decision tool; it is the engine that trains a personalization system running at planetary scale.

03 — The Experiment

What I Built and
How It Works

This project simulates a Netflix-style A/B test entirely in Python. Rather than using real subscriber data, the experiment generates 1,000 synthetic users and replicates the statistical logic Netflix uses internally — random group assignment, behavioral simulation, metric calculation, and hypothesis testing.

The experiment was built in three sequential steps, each one building on the last.

Step 1 — Simulate the Experiment. 1,000 users are created and randomly assigned to either Artwork A or Artwork B. Each user's click behavior is then simulated using the known click probabilities for their assigned artwork — 24% for Artwork A and 30% for Artwork B. Results are stored in a structured data table with three columns: user ID, artwork version, and whether they clicked.
Step 2 — Measure the Outcome. The data is grouped by artwork version and summarized into three business metrics: total users in each group, total clicks generated, and click-through rate. A bar chart visualizes the comparison clearly for non-technical stakeholders.
Step 3 — Test Whether the Result Is Real. A two-proportion z-test is run to determine whether the observed difference in click rates is statistically significant or could reasonably be explained by random chance. A 95% confidence interval is calculated for each group's true click rate to further validate the finding.
04 — The Code

Complete Python Implementation

The full experiment runs in a single script. All three steps — simulation, metric calculation, and statistical testing — are included below exactly as executed in Google Colab.

netflix_ab_test.py 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportions_ztest, proportion_confint

# Set a random seed so results are reproducible
np.random.seed(42)

# ── EXPERIMENT SETUP ──────────────────────────────────────────
n_users       = 1000
artwork_a_ctr = 0.24
artwork_b_ctr = 0.30

# ── SIMULATE THE DATA ─────────────────────────────────────────
user_ids    = np.arange(1, n_users + 1)
assignments = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users)

clicks = []
for i in range(n_users):
    if assignments[i] == 'A':
        click = np.random.binomial(1, artwork_a_ctr)
    else:
        click = np.random.binomial(1, artwork_b_ctr)
    clicks.append(click)

df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id': user_ids,
    'artwork': assignments,
    'clicked': clicks
})

# ── CALCULATE METRICS ─────────────────────────────────────────
summary = df.groupby('artwork')['clicked'].agg(
    total_users='count',
    total_clicks='sum',
    click_through_rate='mean'
).reset_index()

summary['click_through_rate'] = summary['click_through_rate'].round(4)

print("=" * 40)
print("       A/B TEST RESULTS SUMMARY")
print("=" * 40)
print(f"\n{'Artwork':<10} {'Users':<10} {'Clicks':<10} {'CTR':<10}")
print("-" * 40)
for _, row in summary.iterrows():
    print(f"{row['artwork']:<10} {row['total_users']:<10} {row['total_clicks']:<10} {row['click_through_rate']:<10}")

ctr_a = summary.loc[summary['artwork'] == 'A', 'click_through_rate'].values[0]
ctr_b = summary.loc[summary['artwork'] == 'B', 'click_through_rate'].values[0]
lift  = ((ctr_b - ctr_a) / ctr_a * 100).round(2)

print(f"\nRelative lift of Artwork B over Artwork A: {lift}%")
print("=" * 40)

# ── BAR CHART ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
artworks = summary['artwork']
ctrs     = summary['click_through_rate']
colors   = ['#E50914', '#221F1F']

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
bars = ax.bar(artworks, ctrs, color=colors, width=0.4, edgecolor='white', linewidth=0.8)

for bar, val in zip(bars, ctrs):
    ax.text(
        bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
        bar.get_height() + 0.005,
        f'{val:.1%}',
        ha='center', va='bottom',
        fontsize=13, fontweight='bold', color='black'
    )

ax.set_title('Netflix A/B Test — Click-Through Rate by Artwork',
             fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', pad=15)
ax.set_xlabel('Artwork Version', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylabel('Click-Through Rate (CTR)', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylim(0, max(ctrs) + 0.08)
ax.yaxis.set_major_formatter(plt.FuncFormatter(lambda y, _: f'{y:.0%}'))
ax.spines['top'].set_visible(False)
ax.spines['right'].set_visible(False)
ax.set_facecolor('#f9f9f9')
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#f9f9f9')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# ── STATISTICAL TEST ──────────────────────────────────────────
clicks_a = df[df['artwork'] == 'A']['clicked'].values
clicks_b = df[df['artwork'] == 'B']['clicked'].values

users_a        = len(clicks_a)
users_b        = len(clicks_b)
total_clicks_a = clicks_a.sum()
total_clicks_b = clicks_b.sum()

count = np.array([total_clicks_a, total_clicks_b])
nobs  = np.array([users_a, users_b])

z_stat, p_value = proportions_ztest(count, nobs)
ci_a = proportion_confint(total_clicks_a, users_a, alpha=0.05, method='normal')
ci_b = proportion_confint(total_clicks_b, users_b, alpha=0.05, method='normal')

print("\n" + "=" * 40)
print("       STATISTICAL TEST RESULTS")
print("=" * 40)
print(f"\nArtwork A CTR: {ctr_a:.1%}  (95% CI: {ci_a[0]:.1%} – {ci_a[1]:.1%})")
print(f"Artwork B CTR: {ctr_b:.1%}  (95% CI: {ci_b[0]:.1%} – {ci_b[1]:.1%})")
print(f"\nZ-Statistic : {z_stat:.4f}")
print(f"P-Value     : {p_value:.4f}")
print("-" * 40)

if p_value < 0.05:
    print("\n✅ RESULT: The difference IS statistically significant.")
    print("   Artwork B is the clear winner. Netflix should roll it out.")
else:
    print("\n❌ RESULT: The difference is NOT statistically significant.")
    print("   There is not enough evidence to declare a winner yet.")

print("=" * 40)

The raw numbers tell a clear story. Artwork B generated approximately 6 more clicks per 100 users than Artwork A — a roughly 25% relative improvement in click-through rate. But a larger number alone does not make a business case. The critical question is whether that gap is real.

Statistical Test Result: The two-proportion z-test returned a p-value well below the 0.05 threshold, confirming that the difference between Artwork A and Artwork B is statistically significant. The confidence intervals for the two groups do not overlap, providing additional confirmation that these artworks are producing genuinely different outcomes — not just random variation in who happened to click on a given day.

In plain terms: if Artwork A and Artwork B were actually identical in quality, there would be less than a 5% chance of observing a gap this large in a sample of 1,000 users. That is strong enough evidence to act on.

06 — Business Recommendation

What Netflix
Should Do

The experiment produced a clear, statistically validated winner. The recommendation is straightforward: replace Artwork A with Artwork B across all eligible users.

Three factors make this decision easy to justify. First, the performance gap is real — the statistical test rules out luck as an explanation. Second, the relative improvement of approximately 25% in click rate is substantial enough to produce a meaningful business impact at scale. Third, the cost and risk of switching a thumbnail image are essentially zero compared to the viewing hours gained.

At Netflix's actual subscriber scale, a 25% improvement in CTR on a single title across 300 million users represents an enormous increase in total viewing minutes — directly strengthening the core retention metric that drives subscription renewal.

Roll Out Artwork B — The Data Is Conclusive

With a statistically significant p-value, non-overlapping confidence intervals, and a ~25% relative lift in click-through rate, Artwork B is the evidence-backed choice. This is not a close call. In a real Netflix deployment, this result would trigger an automated global rollout with continued monitoring for performance drift across audience segments and devices.
